# Chapter 1 — Freefall

The city was three hundred metres below him, close enough now to make out individual streets, and it looked — this was the strange thing his mind caught on instead of the obvious facts about terminal velocity — it looked peaceful.

The sound was the first thing that surprised him. Not rushing air, not the cinematic whistle he might have expected, but something more total: a roar that swallowed everything, that pressed against his ears and the back of his throat. His shirt had plastered flat against his chest. His eyes watered badly enough that the orange and white lights of Pune below him smeared into something that could have been beautiful if he'd had the presence of mind to think so.

He was tumbling. He windmilled his arms and corrected — corrected, because some part of his body was already treating this as a problem with a solution — and then he was face-down, if that was a meaningful orientation anymore, watching the city grow incrementally larger. The Sahyadri hills were a dark interrupted mass to his west. He could make out, at this distance, the general sprawl of the old city. Shaniwarwada's silhouette. The wide avenue he'd taken to school as a child.

He had perhaps three seconds before the math stopped being theoretical.

He thought — with the portion of his mind that had chosen this moment to become very calm and very clear — *I am not afraid.*

He wasn't. He knew what fear felt like. He'd been afraid for years of a hundred smaller things: of what his visions showed him when he wasn't paying attention, of the space across the dinner table that had stopped being warm, of the particular silence that had settled in the flat like a third person he and Dhara navigated around without naming. He'd been afraid of all of it, continuously, without drama, the way you are afraid of things you cannot fix.

But not this.

He had been falling his whole life. This was just the first time he could see the ground.

Then the wind changed. Or something in him did.

It started in his chest — in that specific place where you feel something you've been carrying for a very long time finally let go, because you chose to let go, or because you'd been holding it long enough that your hands simply gave out — and the sensation spread outward, down his arms, all the way to his fingertips, and the wind caught it, and the wind caught *him*, and —

---

*It had begun two days earlier, on the morning of their parents' death anniversary.*

---

He knew the date before he opened his eyes. He always did. Fifteen years and his body still remembered before the rest of him caught up — a low, specific dread that arrived in the last hour before waking and had nothing to do with dreams.

He lay there for a moment. Looked at the ceiling. Then he got up and made chai.

The gas ring clicked three times before catching. He watched the flame settle and let himself have the small discipline of not reaching for the foresight — some mornings he could afford the defiance of not knowing what was about to happen. The chai smelled like every other morning. The October light through the kitchen window was pale gold.

He heard Dhara's door. Then the bathroom tap.

He poured a second cup and set it at her usual spot.

She came in twelve minutes later with her hair still damp and her bag already on her shoulder — bag on before breakfast, which meant she didn't plan to sit down. She looked at the cup and then at him, in that order.

"You remembered," she said. It was unclear whether she meant the chai or the date.

"Of course," he said.

She picked up the cup. She didn't sit. She stood at the counter and drank half of it quickly, the way you drink something when you intend to be somewhere else soon.

"I'll be back late," she said. "Don't wait."

She left.

He stood in the kitchen for a while after the front door closed, in the particular stillness left by a sound you hadn't realised you were tracking until it stopped.

---

He was on the 8:15 bus to Hinjewadi, hand on the overhead rail, watching Pune move past the window, when the foresight arrived without warning.

It came the way it always did when she was involved — not gently, not with the soft approach of something invited — but suddenly, absolutely, like a door blown open. The bus and the morning traffic were gone and he was somewhere else: the crossing near Fergusson College, afternoon light, the particular angle of October sun at four-thirty. Dhara at the kerb with her bag, looking at her phone.

And the water tanker, coming around the corner at speed, taking the turn wide.

Rihan's hand tightened on the rail.

In the vision Dhara looked up a fraction too late. The probabilities were not ambiguous. Not today. Not on this corner, with this light, with the tanker driver's attention clearly somewhere other than the road.

He reached for his phone without thinking and typed: *Hey. Take the bridge route home today, not the Fergusson crossing. Don't ask why.*

Sent it. Waited.

The three dots appeared. Disappeared. A long pause.

*Fine*, she replied. Nothing else.

He exhaled slowly and put the phone away. At the next stop a woman got on with two small children and he moved to make room, and the city went on through the window, and the foresight went quiet, and he thought — as he always did when it passed — about his parents.

---

He had been sixteen the first time he'd made her chai.

Their parents' kitchen in the east-side house had still been his parents' kitchen then — everything in places he hadn't chosen, a logic to the arrangement that existed in someone else's head and died with them. He'd had to open four different cupboards to find the saucepan.

Dhara had been eight. She'd sat at the table with her schoolbag still on her back, because she hadn't wanted to put it down yet. Keeping it on meant she was just passing through. It meant there was somewhere else to go back to.

The chai had gone wrong. Not enough milk, or too much water, or both.

She'd drunk the whole cup. Held it with both hands the way she held things she wanted to be careful with, and drank it to the bottom, and looked at him after with something in her face that wasn't pity — she'd been eight, she didn't know how to pity yet — but something adjacent to trust.

*This will do*, it said. *You will do. We can do this.*

He had washed the cup and thought: *okay. We can do this.*

---

The workday left no memory. He moved cards on a board. He answered messages. He sat in a standup and contributed three sentences he forgot immediately. He deferred the code review again. He ate lunch at his desk.

By five the hollow feeling had full possession of the day — the feeling that it had been correct and sufficient in every measurable way and utterly, thoroughly empty. On the anniversary it was worse. On the anniversary it had a specific texture: not grief exactly, not after fifteen years, but the awareness of a shape that should be there and wasn't, the way a room feels when furniture has been taken out and you still navigate around where it was.

He took the 6:20 bus home.

The route went past a road he knew. He didn't have to look at the sign to know which one. He'd memorised the turn, the way the curve sat, the particular quality of the evening light when it hit that section of road at a low angle.

He knew it because he had seen it first at fourteen.

Not like seeing — he hadn't understood that yet, hadn't had a word for what it was. He'd been sitting in class and something had moved through him, cold and sudden and specific, and what it resolved into had made no sense. His parents. A road he didn't recognise. The evening light doing something wrong at a curve.

*What is this?* had been his first thought. Then: *Is this real?* And then the thought that would follow him for fifteen years: *What if it is?*

Because what did you do with that? What did a fourteen-year-old do with an image of his parents dying on a road? Who did you tell? His parents — *Amma, I had a feeling, don't go to Nashik, I don't know why, I just — I can't explain it*? And then if nothing happened, then what? If they thought he was ill? If he was ill?

It wasn't real. It couldn't be real. He'd held that thought for six weeks and almost made himself believe it, and then October had come and the road had been exactly where he'd known it would be, the light at exactly the angle he'd seen, and the truck at the curve, and —

He had known. He had known and he had not said one word. He had not known what word to say, or who to say it to, or whether what he'd seen was real enough to act on. He had been fourteen and the world had not given him any framework for this and so he had held it in and held still and the outcome had been exactly what he'd seen.

He had been learning to hold still ever since.

The bus went past the road. He did not look.

---

She was at the chai stall on the corner he passed on the walk home from the bus stop.

Not doing anything notable — sitting at one of the small plastic tables with a glass of tea she hadn't drunk, watching the street. She was somewhere in her early forties, dressed in nothing remarkable. She had very steady eyes. She looked up when he came around the corner without the surprise of someone who had been waiting and was relieved — without any surprise at all.

"Sit down," she said. "I'll get you chai."

"I don't—"

"You don't usually stop here on Thursdays," she said. "You will today."

He stopped. He looked at her. He sat down.

She ordered two cups. Then she folded her hands on the table and looked at him.

"How long has it been," she said, "since a day felt like it was actually happening to you? Rather than happening near you, while you watched?"

He was quiet for a moment. Something about the question was too specific to deflect.

"Four years," he said. "Maybe five."

She nodded, as if this were the answer she expected. "The girl you texted this morning," she said. "Dhara. The bridge route. Why did you tell her that?"

Something went very still inside him.

"She's my sister. I had a feeling."

"Yes. You did." She said it without weight, as if it were simply a fact. "You had the same kind of feeling eighteen months ago, when you called her to get off a bus she'd been on for two stops. Eight months ago, the long way home. Last spring, the thing at the college gate." She listed them without drama. "Each time a feeling. Each time specific. Each time right."

He looked at her.

"I've been watching for six months," she said. "I apologise for the time it took. You're more careful than most — you've kept the interventions small. Easy to dismiss as coincidence, one at a time." She paused. "I stopped dismissing them."

"What do you want?"

"Nothing from you. I have something for you." She picked up her chai and drank some. "But I'll understand if you'd rather not hear it."

He stood up. He wasn't sure when he'd decided to. "I have to go," he said.

"Of course," she said. She didn't move to stop him. "I'll be here tomorrow morning. Same time."

He walked away without responding. He didn't look back. He walked home quickly, let himself in, stood in the kitchen for a moment, and then went to his room.

He did not sleep.

---

He was back at the stall by seven the next morning.

She was already there. Two cups waiting.

"Tell me," he said.

She did. She spoke the way someone speaks when they have given this particular explanation many times and have learned what to give first and what to let the person arrive at on their own. She talked about the wind, and about what it could hold. She talked about people who existed at a different register — built with a closer relationship to air and probability than most. She talked about how abilities like his were not anomalies or illnesses. They were the specific form that a specific nature took.

"The visions," he said.

"The specific form yours takes," she said. "Foresight. Probable futures, not certain ones. You don't see what will happen. You see what might. What changes as people make choices." She tilted her head. "That's why you second-guess yourself. You've always known the future isn't fixed. You've been living with that uncertainty since you were fourteen."

He looked at his cup. "And the other thing."

"What other thing?"

He didn't know how to say it. "When I'm near her," he said. "Dhara. Sometimes it's like the air moves differently. Like something shifts."

"Yes," Tara said. "That's the other part." She was quiet for a moment. "People like you can fly."

He looked up.

"I know," she said.

"You understand what you just said."

"I've been doing this for a very long time. I know how it sounds." She said it without any defensiveness. "I'm not asking you to believe me. I'm asking you to come somewhere with me and see."

"See what?"

"What I can do. What you can do. Whatever questions you have, you can ask them there." She paused. "Then you decide."

He looked at the street outside. At the morning traffic, at a city that was entirely, completely ordinary.

He thought about four years of hollow days. About the foresight, which had been his secret and his weight since he was fourteen, which no one else knew about, which he had never once been able to explain to another person — and this woman had just described it back to him with the calm of someone reading from a document.

"Where?" he said.

"Rajgad. West of the city." She held his gaze. "I'll drive."

---

The city thinned out around them as they headed west, the way cities do when you've finally committed to leaving — gradually and then suddenly, apartment blocks becoming smaller buildings becoming open land becoming the foothills. She drove without talking. He was glad of it. He watched the Sahyadri range come up ahead of them in the afternoon light and tried to arrange what he knew into a shape that would hold.

The road ended. They walked.

The wind up here was different — steadier, deliberate, as if it had a direction it had thought carefully about and committed to. The fort emerged through the trees in stages: a wall, a gate, then the shape of something that had been enormous once and still was, under the ruin. She led him through gates that were more intact than they should have been, up through stone corridors that went higher than seemed right, upward.

The upper citadel opened to the sky.

Pune was below them. Far below — he hadn't registered, while climbing, quite how far. He could make out the approximate area of his flat. He could see the curve of the river. The city looked, from here, like something that could be held in two hands.

"We need to reach the east tower," Tara said.

He followed the direction of her hand. The east tower was perhaps a hundred metres away. Between the citadel and the tower was nothing — open air and the long drop.

"There's no bridge," he said.

"No," she said.

He looked at her. "You want me to fly across."

"I want you to try." She turned to face him, and there was no performance in it, no theatrics — just the steady eyes of someone who meant what they said. "I know what I'm asking. You think it's impossible. I'm not asking you to believe it isn't. I'm asking you to step off the edge and find out."

"What if I fall?"

"You'll fall," she said. "For a moment." She paused. "And then you won't."

"How do you know that?"

"Because I've stood here with eighteen people in the last four hundred years and not one of them hit the ground." She said it simply, as if it were logistics. "And because you've been doing the harder version of this your whole life — seeing something you couldn't prove and acting on it anyway. You texted your sister this morning because you trusted a vision that you had no way to verify. This is the same."

"This is not the same."

"It is," she said. "Precisely the same. The only difference is you'll know the answer in about fifteen seconds."

He stood there. The wind moved across the citadel with its particular deliberateness.

He thought about fourteen years of holding still. About every moment he had kept knowledge to himself because he hadn't known what to do with it, hadn't known who to tell, hadn't been certain enough to act — and the outcome had been what it was. He thought about the hollow feeling, four years deep. He thought about Dhara's door, closing with its particular finality.

He was tired of holding still.

"Alright," he said.

He stepped off the edge.

---

The city was three hundred metres below him and the wind was trying to tear his face off.

He tumbled. Corrected. The orange and white lights of Pune smeared in his watering eyes. The sound was total. He was aware of his heart, its specific location in his chest, the way you are only aware of it when something reminds you that it has been there all along.

He had perhaps three seconds. He used one of them to think — clearly, specifically — about every moment he had been in this exact position: between what he could see coming and the ground. The night his parents died, when he had held a vision in his hands and not known what to do with it and done nothing. The eleven weeks since Dhara's door, when he had stood in the kitchen each morning and not crossed the distance and not said the thing and held still, held still, held still.

He had been in freefall for fifteen years.

Then something in him let go.

He couldn't have said later whether he did it or whether it happened to him. It started in his chest, in the specific place where you feel something you have been carrying for a very long time finally release — because you chose to release it, or because you had been holding on long enough that your hands gave out — and the sensation moved outward from that place, down his arms to his fingers, and the wind caught it, and the wind caught *him*, and the mathematics of the situation changed.

He had been falling.

He was not falling now.

Pune spread below him — all light and sound and motion, a city going about its evening — and the wind was in his hands, and the Sahyadri hills were a dark mass to his west, and the sky was enormous and dark and entirely, shockingly his.

He did not think: *so this is what I am.* He did not think anything at all. He flew, and the city turned below him, and the night came up around him like a door opening onto a room that had always been there — waiting, lit, unhurried — and for the first time in a very long time, something in him was not hollow.
