# Chapter 1 — Freefall

The wind hit him like a wall and the city below was still small enough to look like a map.

He could see the whole of it from here — the orange grid of the old city, the clustered lights of the tech corridors to the northwest, the dark interruption of the Sahyadri hills to the west, the river a dim thread through the middle. Small. Containable. The kind of view you got from planes, from the safe side of glass.

He was not on the safe side of anything.

The sound was the first thing that surprised him. Not the whistle he'd have expected but something more absolute — a roar that filled his ears and the back of his throat and left no room for anything else. His shirt pressed flat against his chest. His eyes tore immediately, badly enough that the city below smeared into something that could have been beautiful if he'd had the presence of mind for it.

He tumbled. Windmilled his arms and corrected — corrected, as if some part of his body had decided this was a problem with a solution — and then he was face-down and the city was no longer a map.

The old city had become a neighbourhood. He could make out the shape of Shaniwarwada's walls now, the specific dark of the open ground around it, the avenue he'd walked as a child. The tech corridors to the northwest were individual buildings, not a cluster. The river had width. The city was growing, the way something grows when you are moving toward it at speed, each second revealing more detail than the last — streets wide enough to count the lanes, traffic signals doing their patient work, the particular red of a bus stop sign.

The wind was getting louder. Or he was getting faster. Both, probably.

He thought — with the portion of his mind that had chosen this moment to become very calm — *I am not afraid.*

He wasn't. He knew what fear felt like. He'd been afraid for years of a hundred smaller things: of what his visions showed him when he wasn't paying attention, of the space across the dinner table that had stopped being warm, of the particular silence that had settled in the flat like a third person he and Dhara navigated around without naming. He'd been afraid of all of it, continuously, without drama, the way you are afraid of things you cannot fix.

But not this.

He could see his neighbourhood now. The general shape of the streets near his building, the flyover where the traffic never moved before seven. He knew where his flat was without being able to see the building specifically, the way you know where something is because you have walked to it so many times that your body has mapped it independently of your eyes. It was coming up at him. Everything was coming up at him.

He had been falling his whole life. This was just the first time he could see the ground.

Then the wind changed. Or something in him did.

It started in his chest — in that specific place where you feel something you have been carrying for a very long time finally let go, because you chose to let go, or because your hands simply gave out — and the sensation spread outward, down his arms, all the way to his fingertips, and the wind that had been tearing at him shifted, and the wind caught it, and the wind caught *him*, and —

---

*It had begun two days earlier, on the morning of their parents' death anniversary.*

---

He always knew the date before he opened his eyes. Fifteen years and his body still remembered before the rest of him — a low, specific dread that arrived in the last hour before waking and had nothing to do with dreams. He lay there for a moment, looking at the ceiling.

Then he got up and made chai.

The gas ring clicked three times before catching. He stood at the saucepan and let himself have the small discipline of not reaching for the foresight — some mornings he allowed himself the defiance of not knowing what was about to happen. The chai smelled like every other morning. The October light through the kitchen window was pale gold.

He heard Dhara's door. Then the bathroom tap.

He set a second cup at her usual spot and sat down with his own and opened his laptop. He had a morning standup at nine. Three tickets to close before end of week. A code review he'd been deferring since Thursday because the engineer who'd written it was going to take the feedback personally, and Rihan hadn't been able to find the energy to manage both the feedback and the feeling.

He closed the laptop.

The flat was small — second floor in a building off FC Road, close enough to the university that Dhara could walk to class, close enough to the bus route that Rihan didn't need a vehicle. Two bedrooms, a kitchen that opened into a room that served as living and dining by turns depending on what the table was being used for. He'd moved them here three years after the parents died. Before that they'd been further east, in a place that was bigger but wrong in a way he couldn't articulate until they'd left.

The new flat had felt like a decision made for the right reasons. He had been twenty-three. He was twenty-nine now, and the right reasons had gotten complicated.

Dhara came in twelve minutes later with her hair still damp and her bag already on her shoulder. Bag on before breakfast meant she didn't plan to sit down. She looked at the cup he'd left at her spot and then at him, in that order.

"You remembered," she said. It was unclear whether she meant the chai or the date.

"Of course," he said.

She picked up the cup. Didn't sit. She stood at the counter and drank half of it quickly, the way you drink something when you intend to be somewhere else.

"I'll be back late," she said. "Don't wait."

She left.

He stood in the kitchen for a while after the front door closed, in the particular stillness left by a sound you hadn't realised you were tracking until it stopped.

---

He took the 8:15 bus to Hinjewadi. It was crowded the way it always was — bodies at the door, someone's bag against his shoulder, the road slow through the Paud Phata junction. He stood with his hand on the overhead rail and watched Pune pass outside the window.

October was almost bearable. The monsoon had finished and the air still had its cleaned quality, the heat of summer not yet returned. Bougainvillea on the compound walls was doing something excessive and purple. An auto driver argued on his phone at a red light. A dog slept in the precise centre of an intersection with the confidence of someone who had made a decision and saw no reason to revisit it.

He was watching the dog when the foresight arrived.

It came the way it always did when she was involved — not gently, not with the soft approach of something invited, but suddenly, completely, like a door blown open in a storm. The bus and the morning traffic were gone and he was somewhere else: the crossing near Fergusson College, afternoon light, the specific angle of October sun at four-thirty. Dhara at the kerb with her bag, looking at her phone.

And the water tanker, coming around the corner too fast, taking the turn too wide.

Rihan's hand tightened on the rail.

In the vision Dhara looked up a fraction too late. The probabilities were not ambiguous. Not today, not on this corner, with this light, with the driver's attention clearly on something other than the road.

He reached for his phone and typed without looking: *Hey. Take the bridge route home today, not the Fergusson crossing. Don't ask why.*

Sent it. Waited.

The three dots appeared. Disappeared. A long pause that felt longer than it was.

*Fine*, she replied. Nothing else.

He exhaled slowly and put the phone away. At the next stop a woman got on with two small children and he moved down to make room, and the city went on through the window, and the foresight went quiet, and he thought — the way he always did when it passed — about his parents.

---

He had been sixteen the first time he'd made chai for her.

Their parents' kitchen in the east-side house had still been his parents' kitchen then — everything in a place he hadn't chosen, a logic to the arrangement that existed in someone else's head. He'd had to open four different cupboards to find the saucepan. Dhara had been eight. She'd sat at the table with her schoolbag still on her back, because she hadn't wanted to put it down yet. Keeping it on meant she was just passing through. It meant there was somewhere else to go back to.

The chai had gone wrong. Not enough milk, or too much water, or both.

She'd drunk the whole cup. Held it with both hands the way she held things she wanted to be careful with, and drank it to the bottom, and looked at him after with something in her face that wasn't pity — she'd been eight, she didn't know how to pity yet — but something adjacent to trust.

*This will do*, it said. *You will do. We can do this.*

He had washed the cup and thought: *okay. We can do this.*

---

He got off at Hinjewadi and walked the ten minutes to the office and sat down at his desk and joined the standup two minutes late, which was on time by the standards of everyone present. Priya from infra wanted to discuss the deployment pipeline. Rihan contributed three sentences he forgot immediately after saying them. He wrote down two action items he was already certain he would defer.

He closed two of the three tickets before lunch. He ate at his desk. He drank two cups of bad coffee from the machine in the corridor. He opened the code review, read the first twelve lines, decided he still didn't have the bandwidth to manage both the feedback and the feeling, and closed it again.

In the afternoon he sat with a JIRA board for forty minutes and moved exactly one card. He answered seven messages. He had a second standup at three-thirty where someone from product wanted an estimate and Rihan gave one that he was approximately forty percent confident in and no one questioned it.

By five the hollow feeling had full possession of the day — the feeling that it had been correct and sufficient in every measurable way and utterly, thoroughly empty. On the anniversary it was worse. On the anniversary it had a specific texture to it: not grief exactly, not after fifteen years, but the persistent awareness of a shape that should be somewhere and wasn't, the way a room feels when the furniture has been taken out and you still navigate around where it was.

He shut his laptop and took the 6:20 bus home.

---

The route went past a road he knew.

He didn't have to check the sign. He'd memorised the turn years ago, the way the curve sat, the quality of the evening light when it hit that section at a low angle. He knew it because he had seen it first at fourteen.

Not like seeing — he hadn't understood that yet, hadn't had a word for what it was. He had been sitting in class and something had moved through him, cold and sudden and specific, and what it resolved into had made no sense. His parents. A road he didn't recognise. The evening light doing something wrong at a curve.

*What is this?* had been his first thought.

Then: *Is this real?*

And then: *What if it is?*

Because what did you do with that? What did a fourteen-year-old do with an image of his parents on a road, in the evening, the light at a specific angle? Who did you tell? His parents — *Amma, I had a feeling, don't go to Nashik, I can't explain it*? And then if nothing happened? If they thought he was unwell? If he was unwell?

It wasn't real. It couldn't be. He'd held that for six weeks and had almost, almost convinced himself — and then October had come and the light had been at exactly the angle he'd seen, the curve exactly where he'd known it would be, the truck at exactly the wrong moment, and —

He had known. He had watched it happen in his own head six weeks before it happened on that road, and he had not said a word to anyone, because he hadn't known which word to say, or who to say it to, or whether what he'd seen was solid enough to act on.

He had been fourteen. He had not known the word for what he was, or what he believed.

He knew both now.

The bus went past the road. He did not look.

---

She was at the chai stall on the corner he passed on the walk home from the bus stop.

Sitting at one of the small plastic tables with a glass of tea she hadn't touched, watching the street with the patience of someone who had somewhere to be and had already arrived. She looked to be in her early forties, dressed in nothing remarkable. Very steady eyes. She looked up when he came around the corner without the surprise of someone who had been waiting — without any surprise at all.

"Sit down," she said. "I'll get you chai."

"I don't—"

"You don't usually stop here on Thursdays. You will today."

He stopped. He looked at her. He sat down.

She ordered two cups from inside. Then she folded her hands on the table and said: "How long has it been since a day felt like it was actually happening to you? Rather than happening near you, while you watched?"

He was quiet for a moment. The question was too specific to deflect.

"Four years," he said. "Maybe five."

She nodded — not as if she was surprised, but as if it were the number she expected. "The girl you texted this morning," she said. "Dhara. The bridge route. Why?"

Something went very still inside him.

"She's my sister. I had a feeling."

"Yes." She said it without weight, as if it were simply a fact that had been established. "You've had that kind of feeling before. Eighteen months ago you called her to get off a bus she'd been on for two stops. Eight months ago, the long way home. Last spring, at the college gate." She listed them without drama, without looking away. "Each time a feeling. Each time specific. Each time right."

He looked at her.

"I've been watching for six months," she said. "I apologise for the time it took. You keep the interventions small. Easy to dismiss one at a time." A pause. "I stopped dismissing them."

"Who are you?"

"My name is Tara." Said plainly, the way you say something that doesn't need decoration. "I have something to tell you. I'll understand if you'd rather not hear it."

He stood up. His legs made the decision slightly ahead of his mind. "I have to go," he said.

"Of course," she said. She didn't move. "I'll be here tomorrow morning. Same time."

He walked home quickly. Let himself in. Stood in the kitchen.

He did not sleep.

---

He was back at the stall by seven the next morning.

She was already there. Two cups waiting.

"Tell me," he said, and sat down.

She spoke the way someone speaks when they've given a particular explanation many times and have learned what to lead with and what to let the person arrive at on their own. She talked about the wind and what it could carry. She talked about people who existed at a different register — built with a closer relationship to air and probability than most. She talked about how the things he'd been experiencing were not anomalies or illness. They were the specific form that a specific nature took.

"The visions," he said.

"The specific form yours takes. Foresight — probable futures, not certain ones. You don't see what will happen. You see what might, as people make choices." She tilted her head slightly. "That's why you second-guess yourself. You've always known the future isn't fixed. You've been living with that uncertainty since you were fourteen."

He looked at his cup. "And the other thing."

"Which other thing?"

He didn't know how to say it. "When I'm near her. Dhara. Sometimes it feels like the air moves differently. Like something adjusts."

"Yes," Tara said. "That's the rest of it." A pause. "People like you can fly."

He looked up.

"I know," she said.

"Do you understand what you just said."

"I've been doing this for a very long time," she said, without any defensiveness. "I know exactly how it sounds. I'm not asking you to believe me. I'm asking you to come somewhere with me and see."

"See what?"

"What I can do. What you can do. Then you ask whatever you need to ask, and then you decide."

He looked at the street outside. Ordinary morning traffic. A city that was, in every visible way, entirely normal.

He thought about the hollow feeling. Four years, he'd said. Maybe five. He thought about the foresight, which had been his secret and his weight since he was fourteen and which no one had ever been able to account for — and this woman had just listed the right incidents back to him in the right order with the calm of someone reading from notes.

"Where?" he said.

"Rajgad. West of the city." She held his gaze. "I'll drive."

---

The city thinned around them as they headed west, the way cities do when you've finally committed to leaving — gradually, and then suddenly, apartment blocks giving way to smaller buildings, smaller buildings to open land, open land to the foothills. She drove without talking. He was glad of it. He watched the Sahyadri range rise ahead of them in the afternoon light and tried to arrange what he knew into a shape that would hold.

The road ended. They walked.

The wind up here was different — steadier, more deliberate, as if it had committed to a direction and seen no reason to reconsider. The fort emerged from the trees in stages: a wall, a gate, then the full shape of something that had been enormous once and still was, underneath the ruin. She led him through corridors where the stone was cold under his hand, upward through levels that should have been more deteriorated than they were, higher.

The upper citadel opened to the sky.

Pune was below them. Far below — he hadn't registered, while climbing, quite how far. He could see the general shape of his neighbourhood. The curve of the river. The city looked, from up here, like something that could be held in two hands.

"We need to reach the east tower," Tara said.

He followed the direction of her hand. The east tower stood perhaps a hundred metres away. Between here and there was nothing — open air, and the long drop.

"There's no bridge," he said.

"No."

He looked at her. "You want me to fly across."

"I want you to step off the edge and find out." She turned to face him fully. No theatrics in it, no ceremony — just the steady eyes of someone who meant exactly what she said. "I know what I'm asking. I'm not asking you to believe it's possible. I'm asking you to try."

"What if I fall?"

"You'll fall," she said. "For a moment. And then you won't."

"How can you know that."

"Because I've stood here with eighteen people across four centuries and not one of them hit the ground." Said simply, as if it were logistics. "And because you've been doing the harder version of this your whole life — seeing something you couldn't prove and acting on it anyway. You sent your sister a text yesterday morning based on a vision you had no way to verify. You've been doing that for years." She paused. "This is the same."

"This is not the same."

"It is exactly the same. The only difference is you'll know the answer in about fifteen seconds."

He stood at the edge. The wind moved across the citadel, steady and purposeful. Below him the city was distant and lit and going about its evening entirely without concern for what was happening on this parapet.

He thought about fourteen years of holding still. Every moment he had watched something coming and kept the knowledge to himself because he hadn't known the right word, or the right person, or whether what he could see was solid enough to act on. He thought about the hollow feeling. He thought about Dhara's door, closing with its particular finality. He thought about a cup of tea drunk to the bottom by an eight-year-old who'd kept her schoolbag on so there was still somewhere to go back to.

He was tired of holding still.

"Alright," he said, and stepped off the edge.

---

The wind hit him like a wall and the city below was still small enough to look like a map.

Then it wasn't.

The old city grew. Shaniwarwada's walls came into focus, then the individual shapes of the buildings around it, then the avenue, then the specific trees that lined it. The river had banks. The tech corridors to the northwest were individual buildings, then individual floors, then lit windows where people were working late. The city was growing toward him the way all things grow when you are moving at speed — revealing more detail with each second, the broad strokes becoming specific, the specific becoming urgent.

The wind was very loud now. His eyes were watering badly. He could feel the acceleration in his stomach, in the backs of his legs, in the pressure against his chest that was increasing not because the wind had gotten stronger but because he was moving through more of it, faster, and the city was right there now — the flyover, the bus stop, the exact road he took every morning — and the mathematics of the situation were not abstract anymore.

He thought: *I have been here before. This exact space — between the thing I can see coming and the ground.*

And then something let go.

He couldn't have said, later, whether he did it or whether it happened to him. It started in his chest, in the specific place where you feel something you have been carrying for a very long time finally release — because you chose, or because you had been holding on long enough that your hands gave out — and the sensation spread outward from that place, down his arms to his fingers, and the wind caught it, and the wind caught *him*, and the city stopped getting closer.

He had been falling.

He was not falling now.

Pune spread below him — all light and motion and ordinary evening — and the wind was in his hands, and the Sahyadri hills were dark to his west, and the sky was enormous, and his.

He did not think: *so this is what I am.* He did not think anything at all. He flew, and the city turned slowly below him, and the night came up around him like a door opening onto a room that had always been there, waiting, lit, unhurried, and for the first time in a very long time, something in him was not hollow.
