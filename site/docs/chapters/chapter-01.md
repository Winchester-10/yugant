# Chapter 1 — Freefall

The city was so far below him that it looked peaceful.

Pune at dusk, the Sahyadri hills a dark silhouette to the west, the old city a tangle of amber and shadow. He could make out the Shaniwarwada towers from here, could trace the wide avenue leading to them, could find — without meaning to — the general direction of his flat. The building was too small to see. But he knew where it was.

He was falling toward all of it.

The wind came up at him like a wall. His eyes tore. Somewhere above him a sound he couldn't identify — his own voice, maybe, or just the air tearing around him — rose and was swallowed. His arms had gone out to his sides without his permission, the way a person reaches for something when the ground disappears, and there was nothing to reach for.

He had perhaps three seconds before the math stopped being theoretical.

He thought — with the portion of his mind that had chosen this moment to become very calm — *I am not afraid.*

He wasn't. That was the strange thing. He had been afraid for years of a hundred smaller things: of what his visions showed him, of the space across the dinner table that had stopped being warm, of the particular silence that had settled between him and Dhara like a third person in the flat. He had been afraid of all of it, continuously, without drama, the way you are afraid of things you cannot name and cannot fix.

But not this. Not falling.

He had been falling his whole life. This was just the first time he could see the ground.

Then the wind changed. Or he did.

Something shifted — not in the air around him but inside his chest, the way a door opens not because it was pushed but because whatever was holding it finally let go. The sensation spread outward from that place, down his arms to his fingers, up into his shoulders, and then —

---

*Twenty-three days earlier, he had been standing in his kitchen making chai.*

---

The gas ring clicked three times before catching. Rihan stood in front of it with the saucepan and watched the flame settle and tried to remember if he had already added the ginger.

He had. He knew he had — knew because thirty seconds from now Dhara was going to walk through the door and the chai was going to smell like something that wasn't a mistake. He saw it happen. Then he watched her actually do it.

She didn't say anything about the chai.

She took her cup without speaking and went to her room and closed the door. Not loudly. With a finality that made loudness unnecessary. He stood in the kitchen for a moment longer than he needed to, then poured his own cup and sat at the table and opened his laptop.

He had a morning standup at nine. Three tickets to close before end of week. A code review he'd been deferring since Thursday because the engineer who'd written it was going to take the feedback personally, and Rihan hadn't been able to find the energy to manage both the feedback and the feeling.

He closed the laptop.

The flat was small — second floor in a building off FC Road, close enough to the university that Dhara could walk to class, close enough to the bus route that Rihan didn't need a vehicle. Two bedrooms, a kitchen that opened into a room that served as living and dining by turns depending on what the table was being used for. He'd moved them here three years after the parents died. Before that they'd been further east, in a place that was bigger but wrong in a way he couldn't articulate until they'd left.

The new flat had felt like a decision made for the right reasons. He had been twenty-three. He was twenty-nine now, and the right reasons had gotten complicated.

*Dhara.*

He didn't reach for the foresight deliberately. He never did, for her — it was more like a reflex, the way you check a clock without meaning to. The vision came quietly: Dhara in her room, cross-legged on the bed, her laptop open to something she wasn't reading. She was going to stay in there until he left for work. She was going to make her own breakfast after he was gone. She had been doing this for eleven weeks.

He let the vision go and finished his chai.

---

The first time he'd made it for her, it had tasted like hot water with ambitions.

She had been eight. He had been sixteen, and their parents' kitchen was still their parents' kitchen — everything in a place he hadn't chosen, the spice tins in an order that made sense to someone who was no longer there. He'd had to open four cupboards to find the saucepan. Dhara had sat at the table and watched him with the careful attention she gave to things she thought might go wrong and was too polite to say so.

The chai had gone wrong. Too much water, not enough milk, or both. He wasn't certain.

She'd drunk the whole cup. Hadn't said a word about it. Just held it with both hands the way she held things she wanted to be careful with, and drank it to the bottom, and looked at him after with something in her face that wasn't pity and wasn't quite gratitude.

*I trust you*, it said. *Even at this.*

He had washed the cup and thought: *okay. Okay.*

---

He took the 8:15 bus to Hinjewadi. It was crowded the way it always was — bodies at the door, someone's bag against his shoulder, the road slow through the junction. He stood with his hand on the overhead rail and watched the city pass.

Pune in October was almost bearable. The monsoon had finished and the air still had its cleaned quality, the dust and heat of summer not yet returned. Bougainvillea on the compound walls. An auto driver arguing on his phone. A dog sleeping in the centre of an intersection with the confidence of someone who had made a decision and saw no reason to revisit it.

The foresight flickered, unbidden — a two-wheeler cutting across the junction two stops ahead, the rider fine, just startled. A near-miss that would resolve into nothing. He let it go. He had learned a long time ago that not everything the foresight showed required a response. Most of it was noise. The world generating its small disasters and recoveries, needing nothing from him.

At work he closed two of the three tickets and deferred the code review again and ate lunch at his desk. He drank two cups of bad coffee. He stared at a board for forty minutes and moved exactly one card.

By five he had the hollow feeling that had been his companion for four years. The feeling that this was the day, and now the day was done, and it had been correct and sufficient in every measurable way and utterly, thoroughly empty.

He took the 6:20 bus home. It went past the university gates. He did not look.

---

He had been fourteen the first time.

Not a vision — not yet. Just a feeling, the way you sometimes know a word is wrong before you can explain why. He had been sitting in class and something had moved through him, cold and certain, and when it resolved it wasn't like seeing. It was like remembering something that hadn't happened yet.

His parents. A road. The particular quality of October light in the evening.

He hadn't understood it. There was nothing to tell — just images, just a feeling, a boy in a classroom with something in his chest he didn't have a name for. He'd told himself it was a waking dream, and kept going, and told no one.

Three months later his parents drove to Nashik for a wedding.

The investigating officer had said it was the truck. Coming around the curve too wide. The road was narrow there and the evening light did something to visibility.

Rihan had known the quality of that light. He had watched it happen inside his own head six weeks before it happened on that road. He had not said one word to anyone. He had not said one word because some part of him — some small, quiet part he had spent fifteen years examining from every angle, looking for the place where it became forgivable — had thought:

*If this is meant to happen, who am I to say otherwise?*

He had been fourteen. He hadn't known the word for what he was, or what he believed.

He knew both now.

The bus went past the university. He did not look.

---

He was half a block from the flat when the woman fell into step beside him.

She appeared the way certain things appear when you are not paying attention — not suddenly, just present in a way she hadn't been a moment before. Plain salwar, nothing remarkable. The unhurried pace of someone who had decided where they were going and saw no reason to perform urgency about it. She looked to be in her early forties. She had very steady eyes.

"You've been generating wind anomalies," she said, "for approximately three years."

Rihan stopped walking.

She stopped too, half a step later, and turned to face him with the patience of someone who had done this before and knew the person always needed a moment.

"I'm not—" he started.

"You are," she said. "The pattern around your sister is particularly consistent. A chai that doesn't boil over when it should. A scooter that moves away from her path. You don't do it deliberately. I know. That's usually how it begins."

He looked at her.

"I've been watching for six months," she said. "I apologise for the length of time. You're more careful than most."

"Who are you?"

"My name is Tara." Stated simply, the way you state something that doesn't need decoration. "There's a word for what you are. I'd like to take you somewhere and tell you what it is, if you're willing."

He should have said no. The correct response — the sane response — was to walk away from a stranger on a Pune street who had just described his private foresight with perfect accuracy. Later he would think about why he hadn't.

He thought the answer was Dhara's door, closing with its particular finality. The hollow feeling. The sense that the day had been correct and sufficient in every measurable way.

"Alright," he said.

---

They took a car west, then a road that left the city behind in stages — apartment blocks giving way to smaller buildings, smaller buildings to open land, open land to the foothills. She didn't explain while they drove. He didn't ask. The Sahyadri range came up ahead of them and the road ended and they walked, and the wind up here was different from the city's — steadier, more deliberate, as if it had committed to a direction.

Rajgad emerged from the trees before he expected it. Even in the early dark it looked like something that had been ruined slowly, over centuries, without regret. She led him through gates that seemed more intact than they should have been, up through corridors where the stone was cold under his fingertips, upward. The wind grew stronger with every level.

The upper citadel opened to the sky. Pune glittered below them. Far below.

Tara stood at the edge and looked at him.

"You already know how to fly," she said. "You've just never stopped falling long enough to find out."

He looked at the edge. Then at her.

"That's not how it works," he said.

"It is, actually." No apology, no softening. "Every Vayu I've ever brought here has had to be shown. The body knows before the mind does. The mind argues. The body doesn't."

"You're going to throw me off a fort."

"I'm going to ask if you trust me. Then I'm going to throw you. There's a difference."

He stood there a moment. The wind moved through the space between them.

He thought about fourteen years of holding still. Every time he had watched something coming and kept the knowledge to himself because holding still was the faithful thing. He thought about Dhara's door, and the eleven weeks, and the silence in the flat that had a shape to it now.

He was tired of holding still.

"Yes," he said. "I trust you."

She threw him.

---

The city was so far below him that it looked peaceful.

He was falling — the wind was the same, his eyes were tearing the same, the ground was doing exactly what the ground does when you are running out of distance to it — and then something shifted. Not in the air. In him.

It started in his chest, in the specific place where you feel something you have been carrying for a very long time finally let go — because you chose to release it, or because you had been holding on long enough that your hands simply gave out. The sensation moved outward, down his arms to his fingers, and the wind caught it, and the wind caught *him*, and —

He had been falling his whole life.

He was not falling now.

Pune spread below him, all light and motion, and the wind was in his hands, and the Sahyadri hills were to his west, and the sky was enormous and dark and entirely, shockingly his.

He did not think: *so this is what I am.* He did not think anything at all. He flew, and the city turned slowly below him, and the night came up around him like a door opening onto something that had always been there, and for the first time in a very long time, something in him was not hollow.
